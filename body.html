functioning and characteristics of the respiratory system

As we saw on the home page, the respiratory system is the set of organs that serve to exchange gases with the environment.

The human respiratory system is made up of the nasal passages, mouth, pharynx, larynx, trachea and lungs, which in turn consist of bronchi, bronchioles and alveoli; and in turn consists of respiratory muscles such as the diaphragm and intercostal muscles.

This system is also responsible for maintaining the balance between acids and bases thanks to the elimination of carbon dioxide in the blood.

In the lung alveoli is where the passive exchange of oxygen and carbon dioxide occurs by diffusion between the gaseous environment and our bloodstream.

The function of the respiratory system is to move volumes of air from the atmosphere to the lungs and vice versa, and all this is possible thanks to a process called "ventilation".

Ventilation is a cyclic process (that is, it is repeated an indefinite number of times) that consists of two stages: inspiration, which is the entry of air into our lungs, and expiration, which is the exit.

Breathing is controlled by our sympathetic nervous system and is capable of increasing the respiratory rate as well as decreasing it and can also cause the bronchial tubes to widen and the pulmonary blood vessels to narrow.
